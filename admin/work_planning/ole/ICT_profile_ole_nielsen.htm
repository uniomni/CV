Australian Government Careers Profile

Dr Ole Nielsen

Dr Ole Nielsen is currently a Computational Scientist in the Risk Research Group of Geoscience Australia. Ole studied his Bachelor and Master of Science at Roskilde University Denmark. His PhD is in Mathematical Modelling and was completed at the Technical University of Denmark. His thesis was titled “Wavelets in Scientific Computing”. Ole wanted his studies to be practically focused and his philosophy was “to involve myself in research and the development of technical or scientific technologies aimed at concrete applications that have a high relevance to society”. His current work in Tsunami Inundation Modelling is closely aligned to this philosophy.

Ole your studies and knowledge of science is very extensive. Could you tell us what your studies involved and what type of work you did whilst studying?
“In Denmark I had studied full time and part time from 1984 to 1990. I studied a variety of discipline areas including general science, biology, media, and communications. It was cross disciplinary and problem oriented in its nature and focused on team and project work. Whilst studying I tutored in computer science. During my studies I changed to a double degree in computer science and mathematics. I also took a year off to work as a sound recording engineer for a recording studio. This included working on the sound engineering team for the Roskilde Festival a large Danish Rock Festival that is held once a year. In 1993 there was a recession and it was difficult to find work in computer science so I applied to undertake a PhD in Mathematical Modelling at the Technical University of Denmark. My PhD was focused on the use of wavelet analysis in scientific computing with applications to fiber optics.

In 1995 I came to the Australian National University (ANU) to undertake a six month student exchange program, and in 1996 I studied for seven months at New York University.

In 1998 I worked for a Danish Computing Company UNI•C which was a Centre for Research and Education in high performance computing. My work focused on the design and development of parallel image analysis algorithms. At the same time I applied to undertake a Post Doctorate Fellowship at ANU as I wanted to go back to Australia. This was funded by the Danish Research Council and my research was in Wavelets and Data Mining. I worked with the CSIRO Enterprise Datamining and the Health Insurance Commission developing tools to analyse very large datasets and helped look for patterns in Medicare and PBS claims. I enjoyed the research and the lifestyle of Canberra, so I decided to stay in Australia and find work here. I also met and fell in love with my wife towards the end of 1999. We met whilst scuba diving at the Great Barrier Reef.

In 2000/2001 I was an independent consultant and the main developer of ‘Pathology Explorer’ WEB data mining applications at CSIRO Mathematics and Information Science.

In 2001 I gained an ANU Research Fellowship with the Mathematical Sciences Institute to research analysis of very high dimensional data. This involved the theoretical side rather than the application side of data mining and enhanced my understanding of the theory behind high dimensional regression, multiscale analysis and surface fitting. Some of the spin offs of my work included DNA matching and software for parallel computing. I focused on the development of new technologies in these areas. When I finished my research studies in 2003 I applied for a position as a Computational Scientist in the Risk Research Group at Geoscience Australia. However, in 2003 after accepting the position at Geoscience Australia, I also honoured a commitment to teach computer science in Thailand at Suranaree University of Technology for months. This work was with PhD students and involved building a LINUX cluster as well as developing parallel algorithms for computational science.

Ole can you tell us how you came to work in Australia for Geoscience?
“In 2003 I started working with Geoscience Australia. It was a natural progression of my career as I wanted to apply science to problems of relevance to society.

In this context I was asked to lead the Inundation Modelling Project. This project revolves around the development of ANUGA a collaborative research and development project with ANU and Geoscience Australia. Its focus is on the development of software modelling tools for hydrodynamic simulations for use with Tsunami Impact Modelling. It brings together spatial information, mathematics, hydrology and computer science and involves the design, coding, testing and application of modeling applications. ANUGA is specifically designed to model the process of coastal inundation and is suitable for modelling structural impacts to infrastructure and to some extent the resulting economic and social consequences.

The software implements the finite volume method of solving the Shallow Water Wave equation. An important capability of the software is that it can model the process of wetting and drying as water enters and leaves an area. This means that it is suitable for simulating water flow onto a beach or dry land and around structures such as buildings. ANUGA is written in object oriented programming language Python which can be readily adapted to changing requirements through out its lifetime allowing for a flexible and easy to use interface. ANUGA has been validated against measured results from a wave tank simulation of the 1993 Okushiri Island Tsunami off Hokkaido. A feature of the Tsunami was a maximum run up of 32 metres observed at the head of the Monai Valley. The wave tank dataset provided bathymetry and topography along with initial water depth and wave specifications. It also contained water depth time series from a wave gauge simulated offshore from the simulation inundation area.

The software is based on almost fifteen years of research at the ANU and three years of development work at GA and will soon be released under an Open Source license. Liaisons with Australian Government Information Management Office (AGIMO) and Department of Education Science and Technology has been very useful for this process ”.

Ole you are working on a very interesting project with ANU and Geoscience Australia. Can you tell us what it is that you enjoy about working in the APS/academic environment?
“I love the collaborative project work and the environment fostered by both ANU and Geosciences Australia. It is wonderful to be able to work with cross disciplinary teams who are very professional and who value and respect each others skills, abilities and experiences. The synergies generated from the combined knowledge and experience is far greater than if we all work in isolation. It also brings greater momentum to the project as we can really achieve some fabulous combined effort results that will benefit the community and society as a whole.

For example, immediately after the recent August Tsunami in Indonesia, Geoscience Australia sent a team of geologists to undertake field measurements at Shark Bay off the coast of Western Australia. Few people know that there was a tsunami wave at Shark Bay at the same time as in Indonesia. By having Geoscience Australia geologists on hand to immediately undertake the field work and collect the data sets, we are now able to use the data sets to simulate the tsunami wave and its impact on the land. It will be invaluable for validating our work and for predicting the future impact on the region if similar tsunamis were to occur. This collaborative professional approach, rather than a competitive approach, is what I enjoy the most about working in Geoscience Australia.

Also, it is only possible to undertake this type of wok in the APS context as industry will not spend the necessary money required for this type of research. The long term strategic vision, and the money required, is only available in the government context”.

Ole, you have talked about what you like with regard to the ANUGA Project. Can you tell us what else it is that the APS environment offers you that keeps you motivated to work at Geoscience Australia?
“There are many things about the APS context that I enjoy. I have mentioned the high degree of professionalism in Geoscience Australia, and this is one of the things that motivates and inspires me to come to work each morning. I am working with many very talented professional staff across a range of science and business related disciplines. I also liaise with the academic world, which I like, and I engage with other government departments and with industry which makes for a very interesting work environment.

Other aspects of the APS that I like include the employment benefits and that I have a job as exciting and challenging as the one I have. There is also a degree of job security in this environment that is hard to find elsewhere. Moreover, there is also the opportunity to undertake ongoing professional development and I am able to access mentors internally as required. Last year I was selected to undertake a professional development program which focused on leadership and succession planning. I found this very valuable as it enabled me to improve my skills in leadership, strategic thinking and high level collaboration, whilst at the same time having access to internal mentors. I have been given opportunities to lead a project team and to work on a number of different projects”.

Praphul Palekar

Praphul Palekar is currently an IT Team Leader with Centrelink. Praphul is originally from India and he came to Australia in 2000 to study his Masters in Information Technology. He first became interested in ICT during his secondary schooling and went on to study a Bachelor and Diploma of Electronics at Mumbai University in India. Praphul is married and has one son.

Praphul you first studied in India, did you also work in India?
Yes I first worked in India for an electronics and computing company. After about a year and half in the company I decided I needed to undertake further study in computing and I wanted to do so overseas. I looked at European programs but they were so expensive and the cost of living was also very high compared to Australia so I applied for the Masters Program at Swinburne University of Technology in Melbourne and was accepted in 2000.

How did you enjoy your time in Melbourne?
I really enjoyed my time in Melbourne at Swinburne University of Technology and whilst studying I tutored in different areas of computing including programming. The Masters Program went for two years and I was very successful and was one of the top students in the course. When I finished the Masters Program, Centrelink had a Graduate Recruitment Campaign in place so I applied for a position with them and was accepted into their graduate program.

I moved from Melbourne to Canberra in 2003. The move was good as all my relocation expenses were covered by Centrelink. My first 15 days of accommodation were also covered which made moving here much easier. The first role I had in Centrelink was as a developer. This work included programming in whole of systems applications and modifications. It was very interesting work and involved many new opportunities for growth and development. After a three months probation I became a permanent staff member and I now have all the benefits associated with being a permanent APS employee.

In 2005 I was given the opportunity to become a team leader and this role involved a lot more in the way of managerial responsibilities. I currently manage a team of five staff and the primary focus of my work is in the area of database development working on Model 204(M204) database using Java Script, XML, Java and J2EE. The projects I work on are developed and written from scratch.

Do you like living and working in Canberra? How are you enjoying working at Centrelink?
Yes I do like living and working in Canberra. Now that I have a family I feel a lot more settled. My life with my family keeps me very busy and I have a few hobbies such as reading and music which also keep me quite busy.

I like working in Centrelink as it provides me with many different opportunities to develop my skills and to work with clients in different areas of Centrelink. I find the variety and the diversity of work and the mix of staff both challenging and rewarding. My current role requires me to work with many different line managers in the business of Centrelink and this has enabled me to gain a better understanding of the different areas of business within Centrelink, their functions and the overall business processes and procedures. It has also enabled me to understand how to best use technology to enhance the business processes of Centrelink.

Some of the technical projects that I have been involved are developing Forte Source Code Management system, Manage project titled “Fontus – M204 IDE”. Fontus is a graphical IDE developed under Eclipse platform for M204 source code development/management.

What would you like to do in the future?
Within Centrelink as Team Leaders we are able to nominate the type of professional development we would like to undertake. In this context I would like to do further business studies, possibly in the area of Project Management as most of my work is project based and this type of qualification would be very useful in my work context. Other possibilities include general business studies as I have the technical expertise for information technology and I would like to enhance and develop my business knowledge and skills more to complement my technical expertise. For me some form of business qualification would be very valuable.

On the personal side, I had set myself the goal of being a manager, own a house, a car, married with a kid, all by the age of 30 and I have achieved all that. I am very pleased with where my life is right now.

Praphul can you tell us what you like about the APS/Centrelink business environment?
Being new to the APS has been a very positive experience. I have been given a great deal of support and mentoring from other staff and I have been able to apply many of the theories I learnt in my course in the workplace. There are many things that I enjoy about the APS business environment. Firstly you are given a range of different opportunities to develop in many different ways. Whilst the work I undertake is very complex, it is also very interesting and rewarding. The work environment is quite flexible and I can work from home if necessary, and this provides me with some flexibility around my family commitments.

I also think the APS environment provides good remuneration for the type of work I undertake. The ongoing benefits of sick leave and annual leave are very generous. There is also the possibility of working for many different government departments if you wish to do so. As technology is a key platform and enabler for government, there will always be scope for me to be involved with and work with new and interesting technology applications and projects in many different work environments.

I would recommend to anyone interested in an ICT career to consider the APS as an employer of choice.
